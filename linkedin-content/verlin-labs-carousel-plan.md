# Verlin Labs — LinkedIn Carousel Redesign (starting from Post #1)

## Why carousels, and where they help vs. hurt

Carousels (LinkedIn "document" posts) are the single best format for repeated brand exposure — every slide the viewer swipes through re-shows the logo, the teal/navy palette, and the wordmark, which single-image posts and text posts don't get. They also outperform single images on dwell time, which LinkedIn's algorithm rewards.

They're *not* better for everything, so here's the split:

- **Mental Model posts → carousels.** These are frameworks — they naturally break into a hook slide, 3–5 content slides, and a close slide. This is the strongest fit and where branding repetition matters most (this is the "flagship" content pillar).
- **Program Spotlight posts → carousels.** Soft-CTA posts benefit from a short narrative arc (problem → session → outcome) across 4–5 slides instead of one image.
- **Career Signal / Field Notes / Founder's Desk → stay text-only.** This is intentional, not a gap: plain text with a strong opening line outperforms image/carousel posts for these opinion/tactical pieces, and mixing in carousels here would blunt the contrast that makes Founder's Desk feel like a distinct, authentic voice. Keeping these text-only also varies the feed rhythm so the whole calendar doesn't feel like a slide-deck feed.

Every carousel below ends on the same close-slide template (logo mark, wordmark, verlinlabs.com/mental-models or /free-session) so repeat viewers start recognizing the sign-off the way they'd recognize an outro.

**Slide design notes (apply to all):** dark near-black background, teal/cyan/indigo accent per the existing brand system, Verlin Labs "V" mark top-left every slide, slide number bottom-right (e.g. "3/7") — small thing, but it signals "swipe" affordance and tells people how much is left.

## Post #4 — Mental Model: The Abstraction Ladder
**Format: 7-slide carousel** · Fri Jul 17

1. **Cover** — "The Abstraction Ladder" / Mental Models · 02
2. "'How does AI work?' is really three different questions — depending on how far down the ladder you go."
3. "Top rung: it predicts the next word. True, but useless for reasoning about it."
4. "Middle rung: a neural network weighing probabilities over patterns in text. Useful for most day-to-day decisions."

7. **Close** — "Knowing which rung you're on is half the battle." → verlinlabs.com/mental-models

## Post #7 — Mental Model: The Feedback Loop
**Format: 7-slide carousel** · Fri Jul 24

1. **Cover** — "The Feedback Loop" / Mental Models · 03
2. "The most dangerous AI systems aren't the inaccurate ones."
3. "They're the ones whose mistakes never make it back to anyone who could fix them."
4. "A feedback loop: output happens → someone notices if it's wrong → that correction changes future output."
5. "Break any link in that chain and errors compound silently."
6. "Before trusting an AI workflow, ask: when this is wrong, who finds out — and does that finding-out ever reach the system?"
7. **Close** — "If the answer is 'no one,' that's the real risk." → verlinlabs.com/mental-models

## Post #10 — Mental Model: The Compression Lens
**Format: 7-slide carousel** · Fri Jul 31

1. **Cover** — "The Compression Lens" / Mental Models · 04
2. "Every AI summary is a compression. And compression always throws something away."
3. "The question is never 'is this summary accurate' — it's 'what did this compression decide didn't matter, and do I agree?'"
4. "A good summary compresses along the axis you care about."
5. "A bad one compresses along whatever axis was statistically convenient for the model — and they can look identical on the page."
6. "Before acting on an AI summary of a contract, a research transcript, a codebase — ask what got compressed out."
7. **Close** — "It's usually the part you actually needed." → verlinlabs.com/mental-models

*Caption:* Every AI summary throws something away. The question is whether you agree with what it chose →
