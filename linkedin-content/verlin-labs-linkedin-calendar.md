## A cadence call, as acting marketing head

You said 1–3 posts/day and left the exact cadence to my judgment — I'd steer away from that. A brand-new page with 1 follower posting multiple times a day reads as spammy, tanks organic reach (LinkedIn's algorithm suppresses over-posting accounts), and 90 posts/month is more volume than can stay high-quality. Recommending **3 posts/week (Mon/Wed/Fri)** instead — consistent, sustainable, and each post gets a real chance to be seen. That's ~13 posts across the next 4–5 weeks below. Happy to scale up once the page has real traction.

## Content pillars

1. **Mental Model** — a bite-sized AI/tech framework, visual card, links to the matching page on verlinlabs.com/mental-models. Most shareable, most on-brand.
2. **Field Notes** — a practical, tactical AI tip (prompt technique, tool-evaluation heuristic). Positions credibility, no hard sell.
3. **Career Signal** — content for people early in their career / still building their skill set, framed around what's changing in hiring and skills — never named explicitly as a target segment.
4. **Founder's Desk** — a sharp, opinionated take in Aman's voice, posted as the Page (not his personal profile). Builds authentic voice.
5. **Program Spotlight** — soft CTA toward the free session or a course track. Kept to roughly 1-in-4 posts so it never feels like a hard sell.

Image posts use the Verlin Labs teal/navy branding pulled directly from the site's design system — logo mark + wordmark, same palette, same typographic feel. Text-only posts (Founder's Desk, Career Signal, Field Notes) intentionally skip images — plain text with a strong opening line tends to outperform image posts for reach on LinkedIn, and it varies the feed rhythm.

## Week 1

### Mon — Mental Model — *The Map Is Not the Territory* [IMAGE]
> Every AI model is a map. Not the territory.
>
> A language model's confidence has nothing to do with its accuracy. It will describe a wrong answer with the same fluency as a right one — because it's not reasoning about truth, it's predicting plausible text.
>
> The moment you start treating AI output as "the map" instead of "the truth," you ask better questions: What did this leave out? What would change if the input changed? Where would this map mislead me?
>
> This is one of the core mental models we teach at Verlin Labs — because the gap isn't in using AI tools. It's in knowing when to trust them.
>

>
> Every course out there teaches "how to prompt ChatGPT." Almost none teach how to tell when the answer is wrong.

> Unpopular opinion: most "AI training" being sold right now teaches fluency, not literacy.
>
> Fluency is knowing which buttons to press. Literacy is understanding what's happening when you press them — where the system is strong, where it quietly fails, and how to reason about outputs you can't fully verify.
>
> Fluency gets outdated every time a new model ships. Literacy doesn't.
>
> After a decade building AI systems inside large organizations, this is the gap we built Verlin Labs to close. Not another tool tutorial — the thinking underneath it.

## Week 2

### Mon — Mental Model — *The Abstraction Ladder* [IMAGE]
> "How does AI work?" is really three different questions, depending on how far down the ladder you go.
>
> Top rung: it predicts the next word. True, but useless for reasoning about it.
> Middle rung: it's a neural network trained on patterns in text, weighing probabilities. Useful for most day-to-day decisions.
> Bottom rung: attention mechanisms, transformer architecture, gradient descent. Useful if you're building the thing.
>
> Most confusion about AI comes from mixing rungs mid-conversation — explaining a business risk with a bottom-rung detail, or making an engineering decision with a top-rung generalization.
>
> Knowing which rung you're on is half the battle.
>
> More frameworks like this → verlinlabs.com/mental-models

### Wed — Field Notes — the "what would change my mind" test [TEXT]
> A quick test before you trust any AI-generated analysis: ask it "what evidence would change this conclusion?"
>
> If it can't give you a real answer — something specific and falsifiable — the output is closer to a plausible-sounding guess than an actual analysis. Good reasoning (human or AI) can point to what would break it.
>
> Takes ten seconds. Catches a surprising amount of confidently-wrong output before it ends up in a deck or a decision.

## Week 3

### Mon — Mental Model — *The Feedback Loop* [IMAGE]
> The most dangerous AI systems aren't the inaccurate ones. They're the ones whose mistakes never make it back to anyone who could fix them.
>
> A feedback loop is simple: output happens → someone notices if it's wrong → that correction changes future output. Break any link in that chain and errors compound silently.
>
> Before trusting an AI-assisted workflow, ask: when this is wrong, who finds out, and does that finding-out ever reach the system?
>
> If the answer is "no one" — that's the actual risk. Not the model's accuracy on day one.
>
> More frameworks like this → verlinlabs.com/mental-models

### Wed — Career Signal — what's actually being tested now [TEXT]
> Talked to a few hiring managers recently about how AI has changed early-career interviews. The pattern was consistent:
>
> Nobody's testing "can you use AI tools" anymore — that's assumed. What's being tested is judgment: can you tell when the AI-generated code has a subtle bug, when the AI-written analysis missed a confound, when the "solution" technically works but is the wrong solution.
>
> The bar moved from tool-use to tool-supervision. Worth building that muscle deliberately instead of hoping it shows up on its own.

### Fri — Founder's Desk — a contrarian take [TEXT]
> "AI will replace [role]" is almost always the wrongframing.
>
> What actually happens: the people who understand how to work with AI — where to trust it, where to double-check it, how to direct it — become dramatically more capable. Everyone else's output starts looking comparatively slower and thinner.
>
> It's not AI vs. humans. It's AI-literate vs. AI-illiterate, and that gap is going to matter more than most job titles over the next few years.

## Week 4

### Mon — Mental Model — *The Compression Lens* [IMAGE]
> Every AI summary is a compression. And compression always throws something away.
>
> The question is never "is this summary accurate" — it's "what did this compression decide didn't matter, and do I agree?"
>
> A good summary compresses along the axis you care about. A bad one compresses along whatever axis was statistically convenient for the model. They can look identical on the page.
>
> Before acting on an AI summary of anything that matters — a contract, a user research transcript, a codebase — ask what got compressed out. It's usually the part you actually needed.
>
> More frameworks like this → verlinlabs.com/mental-models

### Wed — Field Notes — RAG vs fine-tuning, plainly [TEXT]
> Quick version of a question we get a lot: "should we fine-tune or use RAG?"
>
> Fine-tuning changes how the model behaves — tone, format, task-specific reasoning patterns. RAG changes what the model knows — feeding it facts at query time instead of baking them into weights.
>
> If your problem is "the model doesn't know our data," that's RAG, not fine-tuning. If your problem is "the model knows enough but responds wrong," that's fine-tuning, not RAG. Most teams reach for the expensive one first.

>
> That's the actual outcome we're building toward — not speed, judgment.

### Mon — Mental Model — *The Constraint Box* [IMAGE]
> Every AI tool has a box it was built for. The failures that matter are almost always someone using it outside that box.
>
> A model trained for conversational fluency isn't automatically good at arithmetic. A summarizer isn't automatically a fact-checker. A code assistant trained on public repos doesn't automatically know your internal conventions.
>
> Before adopting any AI tool, map its actual constraint box — what it was optimized for — before mapping what you hope it can do.
>
